WHO GUIDELINE: MALARIA DIAGNOSIS AND TREATMENT — RURAL/PRIMARY CARE
Source: WHO Guidelines for the Treatment of Malaria, 4th Edition 2022

ICD-10 CODES:
- B50.9  — Plasmodium falciparum malaria, unspecified
- B51.9  — Plasmodium vivax malaria without complication
- B54    — Unspecified malaria
- B50.0  — Plasmodium falciparum malaria with cerebral complications

CLINICAL FEATURES — Uncomplicated Malaria:
  - Fever (often cyclical) or history of fever in past 24-48h
  - Chills, rigors, sweating
  - Headache, myalgia, malaise, fatigue
  - Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
  - May have: enlarged spleen, mild anemia, mild jaundice
  
SEVERE/COMPLICATED MALARIA (P. falciparum) — REFER URGENTLY:
  Any ONE of the following:
  - Cerebral malaria (impaired consciousness, seizures, coma)
  - Severe anemia (Hb <7g/dL or severe pallor)
  - Respiratory distress (deep breathing, acidosis)
  - Hypoglycemia (glucose <2.2 mmol/L)
  - Circulatory collapse (systolic BP <70mmHg)
  - Abnormal bleeding
  - Hyperparasitemia (>5% red cells parasitized)
  - Hemoglobinuria (black urine)
  - Jaundice (bilirubin >50μmol/L)
  - Acute renal failure (creatinine >265μmol/L)

DIAGNOSIS:
  1. Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT) — preferred in rural settings
  2. Microscopy (gold standard where available)
  3. Clinical diagnosis only if neither available and high suspicion

Empiric treatment based on epidemiology is acceptable in high-transmission areas when tests unavailable.

TREATMENT — Uncomplicated Falciparum Malaria:
FIRST LINE: Artemisinin-based Combination Therapy (ACT) — based on national guideline:
  Option 1: Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL)
    Weight-based dosing, twice daily × 3 days with fat (improves absorption)
    Adult (≥35kg): 4 tablets BD × 3 days at 0h, 8h, 24h, 36h, 48h, 60h
  
  Option 2: Artesunate-Amodiaquine (ASAQ)
    Adult: 200mg/540mg OD × 3 days
  
  Option 3: Dihydroartemisinin-Piperaquine (DHP)
    Adult: weight-based OD × 3 days

  ⚠ DO NOT USE oral artemisinin monotherapy — promotes resistance

Add Primaquine 0.25mg/kg single dose (for P. falciparum gametocyte clearance; check G6PD if possible)

TREATMENT — Uncomplicated P. vivax Malaria:
  Chloroquine 25mg base/kg over 3 days (10mg/kg day 1 & 2, 5mg/kg day 3) — if CQ-sensitive area
  OR ACT if CQ-resistant vivax (check national policy)
  PLUS Primaquine 0.25-0.5mg/kg/day × 14 days (radical cure; contraindicated in G6PD deficiency, pregnancy)
  
G6PD testing recommended before Primaquine; if unavailable, use with caution and monitor for hemolysis.

TREATMENT — Severe Malaria (pre-referral):
  IV/IM Artesunate (PREFERRED):
    Adult: 2.4mg/kg IV at 0h, 12h, 24h, then daily
    If IV not available: IM Artesunate or IM Artemether
  
  If artesunate unavailable: Quinine IV 20mg/kg loading dose over 4h infusion, then 10mg/kg TDS
  
  Pre-referral rectal artesunate (10mg/kg) if IV not possible and patient unable to take oral.

FEVER MANAGEMENT:
  Paracetamol 1g QDS PRN (avoid aspirin — platelet effects)
  Tepid sponging for high fever

ANTIMALARIAL DRUGS — PREGNANCY:
  First trimester: Quinine + Clindamycin × 7 days (ACT if no alternative)
  Second/Third trimester: ACT (Artemether-Lumefantrine preferred)
  Avoid Primaquine in pregnancy

PREVENTION — RURAL CONTEXT:
  - Insecticide-treated bed nets (ITN) — universal coverage
  - Indoor residual spraying (IRS)
  - Intermittent preventive treatment in pregnancy (IPTp): SP (Sulfadoxine-Pyrimethamine) from 13 weeks, monthly
  - Seasonal malaria chemoprevention (SMC) in Sahel: monthly SP+AQ for children

WHEN TO REFER:
  - Severe/complicated malaria (see above)
  - Failure to respond to first-line ACT within 48h
  - Recurrence within 28 days of treatment
  - Pregnancy with severe malaria
  - Very young children (<5kg) or infants

PATIENT EDUCATION:
  - Complete full 3-day course of ACT even if feeling better
  - Bed nets every night
  - Return immediately if: convulsions, unconsciousness, unable to take medications, worsens after 48h
  - Pregnant women: attend ANC for IPTp doses
